# 5W1H Framework for Research Ideation

## Overview

The 5W1H framework is a systematic thinking tool that helps researchers conceptualize and evaluate research ideas from multiple dimensions. By answering 6 key questions, you can quickly build a comprehensive understanding of a research topic.

## Framework Components

### What

**Core question**: What problem or phenomenon is being studied?

**Thinking dimensions**:
- What is the specific research subject?
- What is the core problem to be solved?
- What are the scope and boundaries of the research?
- What are the expected research outcomes?

**Examples**:
- "Study the interpretability of the Transformer model"
- "Explore few-shot learning for medical image analysis"
- "Analyze sample efficiency problems in reinforcement learning"

### Why

**Core question**: Why is this problem important?

**Thinking dimensions**:
- What is the academic value of this problem?
- What is the practical significance of solving it?
- What are the limitations of current research?
- Why is now a good time to study this problem?

**Evaluation criteria**:
- **Academic importance**: Does it advance the field?
- **Practical impact**: Does it solve real problems?
- **Timeliness**: Does it align with current research trends?
- **Feasibility**: Are there sufficient resources and technical support?

### Who

**Core question**: Who are the target audiences and stakeholders?

**Thinking dimensions**:
- Who will care about this research?
- Who will use the research results?
- Who will benefit from it?
- Who needs to be collaborated with?

**Stakeholder categories**:
- **Academia**: Researchers in the same field, reviewers, citing authors
- **Industry**: Product teams, engineers, decision makers
- **Users**: End users, affected groups
- **Collaborators**: Advisors, colleagues, interdisciplinary experts

### When

**Core question**: What is the time scope and context of the research?

**Thinking dimensions**:
- What is the time span of the research?
- When is the best time to conduct the research?
- When will the required technologies and data be available?
- When are results expected?

**Time planning**:
- **Short-term** (3-6 months): Initial exploration, literature review, method validation
- **Medium-term** (6-12 months): Full experiments, paper writing, submission
- **Long-term** (1-2 years): Systematic research, multiple papers, theoretical contributions

### Where

**Core question**: What are the application scenarios and domains of the research?

**Thinking dimensions**:
- In which specific scenarios is the research applied?
- In which fields or industries is it valuable?
- Does geographic or cultural context matter?
- Where are the experiments conducted?

**Application scenario categories**:
- **Academic scenarios**: Theory validation, method comparison, benchmark testing
- **Industrial scenarios**: Product development, system optimization, problem diagnosis
- **Social scenarios**: Policy making, public services, social impact
- **Cross-domain**: Interdisciplinary crossover, technology transfer

### How

**Core question**: What is the initial research methodology?

**Thinking dimensions**:
- What research methods or technical approaches will be used?
- What data and resources are needed?
- How will research hypotheses be validated?
- What are the expected technical challenges?

**Method types**:
- **Theoretical research**: Mathematical proofs, theoretical analysis, model derivation
- **Experimental research**: Algorithm design, experimental validation, performance evaluation
- **Applied research**: System development, case studies, user testing
- **Comprehensive research**: Literature review, meta-analysis, systematic evaluation

## Usage Examples

### Example 1: Transformer Interpretability Research

**What**: Study the interpretability of attention mechanisms in Transformer models
**Why**: Improve model transparency, increase user trust, meet regulatory requirements
**Who**: NLP researchers, model developers, regulatory bodies
**When**: 6-12 months; Transformers are widely used but lack explanation
**Where**: Text classification, machine translation, QA systems, and other NLP tasks
**How**: Design visualization tools, analyze attention weights, conduct ablation experiments

### Example 2: Few-Shot Learning for Medical Imaging

**What**: Explore the effectiveness of few-shot learning for medical image diagnosis
**Why**: Medical data annotation is costly; few-shot learning reduces data requirements
**Who**: Medical AI researchers, radiologists, hospitals
**When**: 12-18 months, as few-shot learning technology matures
**Where**: X-rays, CT scans, MRI, and other medical imaging diagnosis
**How**: Meta-learning, transfer learning, data augmentation, validated on real medical datasets

## Best Practices

### 1. Iterative Thinking

Do not expect to answer all questions in one pass. Quickly go through all 6 questions first, then iterate and refine repeatedly.

### 2. Priority Ordering

Adjust focus according to research stage:
- **Early stage**: Focus on What, Why, Who
- **Middle stage**: Focus on How, When, Where
- **Late stage**: Review all dimensions comprehensively

### 3. Team Collaboration

Discuss 5W1H questions with advisors and colleagues to get multi-angle feedback.

### 4. Documentation

Record the 5W1H analysis results as the basis for the research proposal.
