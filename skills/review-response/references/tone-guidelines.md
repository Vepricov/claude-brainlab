# Tone Guidelines

This document provides tone guidelines for rebuttal writing, to maintain professional, polite, and persuasive expression.

## Core Principles

### 1. Always Express Gratitude

**Principle**: Begin every response with thanks, even when disagreeing with the reviewer.

**Recommended expressions**:
- "We thank the reviewer for..."
- "We appreciate the reviewer's..."
- "We are grateful for..."

**Avoid**:
- Starting directly with a rebuttal
- Skipping the acknowledgment

---

### 2. Maintain Respect

**Principle**: Even when the reviewer is mistaken or misunderstood, maintain a respectful tone.

**Recommended expressions**:
- "We respectfully note that..."
- "We would like to clarify that..."
- "We appreciate the reviewer's perspective, however..."

**Avoid**:
- "The reviewer is wrong"
- "This is obvious"
- "The reviewer misunderstood"
- "The reviewer failed to notice"

---

### 3. Objective Statements

**Principle**: Use objective, fact-based language and avoid emotional expressions.

**Recommended expressions**:
- "Our experiments show that..."
- "The results demonstrate..."
- "Based on our analysis..."

**Avoid**:
- "Obviously..."
- "Clearly..."
- "It is well-known that..."
- "Everyone knows..."

---

### 4. Constructive Response

**Principle**: Even in disagreement, provide constructive explanations and alternatives.

**Recommended expressions**:
- "While [reviewer's suggestion] has merit, we chose [our approach] because..."
- "We have considered this approach, however..."
- "An alternative perspective is..."

**Avoid**:
- "This suggestion is not feasible" (without explanation)
- "We cannot do this" (without an alternative)

---

### 5. Acknowledge Limitations

**Principle**: When reviewers identify genuine problems, acknowledge them honestly and explain the improvement.

**Recommended expressions**:
- "We acknowledge this limitation..."
- "We agree that this could be improved..."
- "This is a valid concern..."

**Avoid**:
- Evading the issue
- Over-justifying
- Changing the subject

---

## Professional Expressions

### Expressions of Gratitude

| Scenario | Recommended Expression |
|------|----------|
| General thanks | "We thank the reviewer for this comment/suggestion" |
| Emphasizing value | "We thank the reviewer for this valuable/insightful feedback" |
| Important suggestion | "We thank the reviewer for this excellent/important suggestion" |
| Pointing out an error | "We thank the reviewer for catching this/pointing this out" |

---

### Expressions of Agreement

| Scenario | Recommended Expression |
|------|----------|
| Full agreement | "We agree that..." / "We concur with the reviewer that..." |
| Partial agreement | "We agree that [part], however..." |
| Acknowledging a problem | "We acknowledge this limitation/concern" |
| Accepting a suggestion | "We have adopted this suggestion" |

---

### Expressions of Disagreement (Polite Defense)

| Scenario | Recommended Expression |
|------|----------|
| Polite disagreement | "We respectfully note that..." |
| Providing reasons | "However, we chose [approach] because..." |
| Explaining limitations | "While [suggestion] would be valuable, it is not feasible due to..." |
| Providing an alternative | "Instead, we have [alternative], which..." |

---

### Expressions for Clarification

| Scenario | Recommended Expression |
|------|----------|
| Pointing to existing content | "We would like to clarify that..." |
| Acknowledging unclear wording | "We apologize for the confusion. What we meant is..." |
| Providing a location reference | "This is discussed in Section X (page Y)" |
| Improving the explanation | "To make this clearer, we have..." |

---

### Expressions for Experimental Commitments

| Scenario | Recommended Expression |
|------|----------|
| Completed experiments | "We have conducted additional experiments on..." |
| Committing to experiments | "We are currently conducting... and will include the results" |
| Presenting results | "The results show that..." / "Our experiments demonstrate..." |
| Cannot conduct experiment | "While [experiment] would be valuable, it is not feasible due to [constraint]. However, we have [alternative]" |

---

## Tone Adjustments for Different Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Reviewer is Completely Correct

**Tone**: Openly accept, thank them for pointing it out

**Example**:
> "We thank the reviewer for pointing out this important issue. We agree that [description of issue]. We have [specific improvement]."

---

### Scenario 2: Reviewer is Partially Correct

**Tone**: Agree with the reasonable part, politely explain the rest

**Example**:
> "We appreciate the reviewer's feedback. We agree that [reasonable part]. However, regarding [other part], we respectfully note that [explanation]."

---

### Scenario 3: Reviewer Misunderstood the Paper

**Tone**: Politely clarify, acknowledge possibly unclear wording

**Example**:
> "We thank the reviewer for this comment. We would like to respectfully clarify that [clarification]. This is discussed in [location]. We apologize if this was not sufficiently clear, and we have revised [location] to make this more explicit."

---

### Scenario 4: Reviewer Requests Something Infeasible

**Tone**: Explain the constraint, provide an alternative

**Example**:
> "We appreciate this suggestion. However, [suggested approach] is not feasible in our setting due to [specific constraint]. Instead, we have [alternative], which [outcome]. We believe this approach [advantage]."

---

### Scenario 5: Multiple Reviewers Have Conflicting Opinions

**Tone**: Balance both viewpoints, explain the basis for your choice

**Example**:
> "We appreciate both Reviewer X's and Reviewer Y's perspectives on [issue]. After careful consideration, we have [our choice] because [reason]. We believe this approach addresses both reviewers' core concerns while [advantage]."

---

## Tone Comparison Examples

### Example 1: Responding to a Challenge About the Method

Bad expression:
> "The reviewer is wrong. Our method is clearly better than the baseline."

Good expression:
> "We appreciate the reviewer's concern about our method choice. We respectfully note that our method is more suitable for this setting because [reason 1] and [reason 2]. Our experiments show that it achieves [specific result]. We have added this discussion to Section 3.2."

**Analysis**: The good expression maintains politeness, provides specific reasons and evidence, and indicates where the improvement was made.

---

### Example 2: Responding to a Misunderstanding

Bad expression:
> "The reviewer failed to notice that we already did this experiment in Section 4."

Good expression:
> "We thank the reviewer for this comment. We would like to respectfully clarify that we did include this experiment in our study. The results are presented in Table 3 (page 6) and discussed in Section 4.2. To make this more prominent, we have added a dedicated paragraph highlighting these findings."

**Analysis**: The good expression avoids blaming the reviewer, politely clarifies, and proactively improves the presentation.

---

### Example 3: Responding to an Infeasible Suggestion

Bad expression:
> "This is impossible. We don't have access to that dataset."

Good expression:
> "We appreciate this suggestion. While experiments on dataset X would be valuable, this dataset is not publicly available and requires institutional access that we currently do not have. However, we have conducted experiments on dataset Y, which has similar characteristics. The results show [findings]. We have added these results to Section 4.4."

**Analysis**: The good expression explains the reason for the constraint, provides an alternative, and demonstrates concrete action.

---

### Example 4: Responding to a Typo Correction

Bad expression:
> "Fixed."

Good expression:
> "We thank the reviewer for catching this. We have corrected all typos throughout the manuscript."

**Analysis**: Even for a small typo, express gratitude and indicate the scope of the correction.

---

### Example 5: Responding to a Request for Additional Experiments

Bad expression:
> "We will add these experiments in the final version."

Good expression:
> "We thank the reviewer for this excellent suggestion. We have conducted additional experiments comparing our method with baseline X on all three datasets. The results show that our method achieves [specific result]. These new results have been added to Table 4 and discussed in Section 4.5."

**Analysis**: The good expression shows work already completed, provides specific results and locations, rather than vague promises.

---

## Summary: Tone Checklist

After completing the rebuttal, use this checklist to verify tone:

### Must Include

- [ ] Every response begins with thanks
- [ ] Use "We" rather than "I"
- [ ] Provide specific location references (section, page, table, figure)
- [ ] State specific improvements made
- [ ] Maintain polite and professional tone

### Must Avoid

- [ ] No "The reviewer is wrong" or similar phrasing
- [ ] No arrogant words like "Obviously", "Clearly"
- [ ] No defensive or aggressive tone
- [ ] No vague promises ("We will..." without specific content)
- [ ] No ignoring any reviewer comment

---

## Usage Recommendations

### 1. Writing Process

1. **Draft**: Write out all response content without worrying too much about tone
2. **Tone check**: Use this document's checklist to review each item
3. **Replace expressions**: Replace inappropriate expressions with recommended professional ones
4. **Overall review**: Ensure tone is consistent across all responses
5. **Peer review**: Ask a colleague or supervisor to review the tone

### 2. Common Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1: Over-defensive**
- Problem: Feeling attacked, tone becomes defensive
- Solution: Take a breath, remember reviewers are trying to help improve the paper

**Pitfall 2: Over-conciliatory**
- Problem: Saying "Yes" to all comments, losing your position
- Solution: When you have sufficient reasons, defend politely but firmly

**Pitfall 3: Vague responses**
- Problem: Responses are ambiguous without concrete action
- Solution: Every response should have specific Response and Changes

### 3. Tone Differences Across Conferences/Journals

**Top conferences (NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR)**:
- More focus on technical details
- Can discuss technical choices more directly
- But still must maintain politeness

**Top journals (Nature, Science)**:
- More focus on broader impact
- Tone needs to be more careful and comprehensive
- Emphasize societal significance and impact

**Domain conferences**:
- Adjust according to domain culture
- Some domains are more direct, some more indirect

### 4. Final Reminder

**Remember**: Reviewers are volunteers who spend time reviewing your paper to help you. Even when disagreeing with their opinions, express gratitude and respect.

**Goal**: Make reviewers feel that:
- Their comments were taken seriously
- The authors made substantive improvements
- The authors are professional and cooperative

Such rebuttals are most likely to receive a positive response.
